Jupiter

Overview

Jupiter is the largest planet in our solar system, a massive gas giant that holds more than twice the mass of all the other planets combined. Its thick atmosphere is mostly hydrogen and helium, with visible cloud bands and powerful storms, including the Great Red Spot, which has been raging for centuries. Jupiter's immense size generates a strong magnetic field, which is the largest of any planet in the solar system. The planet has over 79 moons, with four large Galilean moons—Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto—being of particular interest due to their geological and possible astrobiological activity. Jupiter’s immense gravitational pull influences the orbits of other celestial bodies, acting as a protective shield for inner planets.

Great Red Spot

Jupiter's most famous feature is the Great Red Spot, a massive storm that has been raging for at least 400 years. This storm, larger than Earth itself, is one of the largest and most powerful weather systems in the solar system. It is located in Jupiter's southern hemisphere and continues to be a key target of scientific observation.

Atmosphere and Composition

Jupiter’s atmosphere is composed primarily of hydrogen and helium, with traces of ammonia, methane, and other gases. Its dynamic atmosphere produces intense storms, high-speed winds, and shifting cloud bands that span thousands of miles. The planet's atmosphere also contains unique features like the Aurora Borealis at its poles.

Magnetic Field

Jupiter has the strongest magnetic field of any planet in the solar system. Its powerful magnetosphere extends millions of kilometers into space, protecting the planet from solar winds and capturing charged particles that form intense radiation belts. This magnetic field is key to understanding Jupiter’s interaction with its moons.

Moons of Jupiter

Jupiter has 79 known moons, the four largest of which are the Galilean moons: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto. These moons are of great interest to astronomers due to their unique geological features and the potential for life, especially on Europa, which is thought to have a subsurface ocean beneath its icy crust.

Exploration and Missions

Jupiter has been explored by missions such as NASA's Juno spacecraft, which is currently studying the planet’s atmosphere and magnetic field. The Galileo spacecraft, launched in 1989, provided a detailed study of Jupiter’s moons and their geological activity. Future missions may involve deeper investigations into Europa’s ice-covered ocean to search for signs of life.

Jupiter with Aurorae

Astronomers are using the Hubble Space Telescope to study Jupiter's auroras, stunning light displays near the planet's magnetic poles. These auroras are produced when high-energy particles, interacting with gas atoms in the atmosphere, create bright ultraviolet glows. The observations, led by Jonathan Nichols of the University of Leicester, aim to understand how Jupiter's auroras respond to variations in the solar wind. This research is complemented by NASA's Juno spacecraft, which is simultaneously measuring solar wind properties as it approaches Jupiter, marking a collaborative effort between the space probe and the telescope.

NASA’s Webb Discovers New Feature in Jupiter’s Atmosphere

NASA’s James Webb Space Telescope has discovered a new, never-before-seen feature in Jupiter’s atmosphere. The high-speed jet stream, which spans more than 3,000 miles (4,800 kilometers) wide, sits over Jupiter’s equator above the main cloud decks. The discovery of this jet is giving insights into how the layers of Jupiter’s famously turbulent atmosphere interact with each other, and how Webb is uniquely capable of tracking those features.
