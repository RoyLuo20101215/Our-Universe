Pluto

Overview

Pluto, once considered the ninth planet, was reclassified as a dwarf planet in 2006 due to its small size and its inability to clear its orbit of other objects. Despite this, Pluto has a rich history in space exploration, having been the subject of NASA’s New Horizons mission in 2015, which provided the first detailed images of its surface. Pluto is composed of ice and rock, with a surface marked by vast plains and mountainous regions. Its atmosphere is thin and primarily made of nitrogen, with methane and carbon monoxide. The planet’s moon, Charon, is relatively large compared to Pluto, and the two bodies are so close that they are often considered a binary system.

History and Demotion

Pluto was discovered in 1930 and initially classified as the ninth planet in the solar system. However, in 2006, it was reclassified as a dwarf planet by the International Astronomical Union due to its small size and inability to clear its orbit of other objects. This led to a significant rethinking of what constitutes a planet.

Surface and Compositione

Pluto’s surface is a mix of ice and rock, and it displays various geological features such as mountains, plains, and craters. Its heart-shaped feature, known as Tombaugh Regio, is an area of vast nitrogen ice plains. Pluto has a thin atmosphere composed of nitrogen, methane, and carbon monoxide.

Orbital Path

Pluto has an elliptical orbit that takes it closer to the Sun than Neptune for a portion of its orbit. This happens once every 248 years, with Pluto spending about 20 years inside Neptune’s orbit. Despite this, the two bodies never collide due to a gravitational resonance that keeps them separated.

Missions to Pluto

NASA's New Horizons mission provided the most detailed images of Pluto to date. In 2015, the spacecraft conducted a flyby, revealing its complex surface and moon system. This mission gave scientists new insights into the planet's atmosphere, geology, and even its moons.

Moons of Pluto

Pluto has five known moons, with Charon being the largest and most notable. Charon is so large that the Pluto-Charon system is sometimes referred to as a double dwarf planet. The other moons, Nix, Hydra, Kerberos, and Styx, are much smaller and more distant from Pluto.

The Changing Faces of Pluto

This is the most detailed view to date of the entire surface of the dwarf planet Pluto, as constructed from multiple NASA Hubble Space Telescope photographs taken from 2002 to 2003. Hubble's view isn't sharp enough to see craters or mountains, if they exist on the surface, but Hubble reveals a complex-looking and variegated world with white, dark-orange, and charcoal-black terrain. The overall color is believed to be a result of ultraviolet radiation from the distant Sun breaking up methane that is present on Pluto's surface, leaving behind a dark, molasses-colored, carbon-rich residue. The center disk (180 degrees) has a mysterious bright spot that is unusually rich in carbon monoxide frost. This region will be photographed in the highest possible detail when NASA's New Horizons probe flies by Pluto in 2015. The Hubble images are a few pixels wide. But through a technique called dithering, multiple, slightly offset pictures can be combined through computer-image processing to synthesize a higher-resolution view than could be seen in a single exposure. This series of pictures took four years and 20 computers operating continuously and simultaneously to accomplish.
