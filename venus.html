Venus

Overview

Venus is often called Earth's "sister planet" due to its similar size and composition, but its environment is vastly different. With a thick, toxic atmosphere made mostly of carbon dioxide, Venus experiences the hottest surface temperatures of any planet in our solar system, even hotter than Mercury. This intense heat, along with pressure 90 times that of Earth, makes the planet's surface inaccessible to most spacecraft. Venus's dense cloud cover reflects much of the sunlight, giving it its bright, golden appearance. Venus rotates slowly and in the opposite direction to most other planets, making its day longer than its year.

Atmosphere and Climate

Venus is shrouded in a thick atmosphere composed mainly of carbon dioxide and sulfuric acid clouds, which create an intense greenhouse effect. As a result, Venus is the hottest planet in our solar system, with surface temperatures exceeding 460°C, even hotter than Mercury, which is closer to the Sun.

Surface and Volcanism

Venus' surface is covered in vast plains, mountains, and large volcanoes. While there is still debate, some scientists believe that volcanism could be active on Venus. The Coronae structures, believed to be volcanic in origin, suggest that internal processes are still at work.

Rotation and Day/Night Cycle

Unlike most planets, Venus rotates in the opposite direction (retrograde rotation) to most other planets. A single day on Venus (the time it takes to complete one full rotation) lasts 243 Earth days, whereas a Venusian year (one full orbit) is only 225 Earth days.

Exploration Missions

Venus has been a target of space missions for decades. The Venera program (Soviet Union) in the 1960s and 70s provided the first images of Venus' surface. More recently, NASA’s Magellan mission mapped Venus' surface with radar, revealing geological features like volcanoes, mountain ranges, and deep canyons.

Challenges for Exploration

The thick atmosphere of Venus presents enormous challenges to exploration. The crushing surface pressure is 92 times greater than Earth's, and temperatures can melt most spacecraft. Recent missions like Akatsuki and future ones, including VERITAS, aim to better understand Venus' climate history and its potential for past habitability.
