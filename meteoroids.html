Meteoroids, Meteors & Meteorites

Overview

Meteoroids are small particles from comets or asteroids that travel through space. When they enter Earth's atmosphere and vaporize, they become meteors or "shooting stars." If they survive their fiery descent and reach the ground, they are known as meteorites. Each stage reveals different aspects of their journey and composition, enriching our knowledge of the solar system’s debris.

History

Historical accounts of meteor showers date back centuries, but it wasn’t until the 19th century that scientists began to link them to comets and asteroids. Meteorites have been studied for their mineral content, and some contain ancient material predating the Sun. These “fossils” from space offer a tangible record of the early solar system.

Formation

Meteoroids are typically fragments from comets or asteroids, broken off during collisions or the Sun’s heat. Once in Earth’s atmosphere, the intense friction causes them to heat up and emit light, creating meteors. If they reach the ground, meteorites provide a sample of material from the asteroid belt or cometary debris.

Current Investigations and Research

Today, scientists analyze meteorites for organic compounds and isotopic ratios, hoping to uncover clues about the early solar system and the origins of life. Research into meteors and meteor showers also aids in predicting when and where these phenomena may occur, expanding our understanding of the solar system’s dynamic processes.
