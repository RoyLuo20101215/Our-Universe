International Space Station

Overview

The International Space Station (ISS) is an orbiting research facility that serves as a unique laboratory for scientific research, international cooperation, and human exploration of space. Continuously inhabited since November 2000, the ISS orbits about 400 km above Earth’s surface, allowing researchers to conduct studies in microgravity across disciplines like biology, physics, astronomy, and meteorology. Its collaborative nature, bringing together efforts from space agencies worldwide, makes it one of humanity's most significant achievements in space exploration and an essential platform for testing technologies and systems that will support future long-term space missions.

History

The origins of the ISS can be traced back to the 1980s, with initial proposals for an international space station coming from both the United States and the Soviet Union. NASA's "Freedom" space station program merged with Russia’s Mir-2 project after the Cold War, pooling resources to create a single, expansive platform in orbit. The formal agreement to build the ISS was signed in 1998 by 15 nations, including the United States, Russia, Japan, Canada, and members of the European Space Agency. Assembly of the ISS began in 1998 with the launch of Zarya, the first module, and continued with contributions from partner nations over the next two decades. Since its inception, the ISS has hosted astronauts from around the world, contributing to groundbreaking scientific discoveries and advancing human knowledge of life in space.

How It's Built

The ISS is constructed from multiple modules, each with specialized functions, which were launched separately and assembled in orbit. These modules include living quarters, laboratories, and other essential facilities, connected to form a continuous, habitable space. The first module, Zarya, provides essential life-support systems, while other notable additions, like the US-built Destiny lab and Japan's Kibo module, serve as research spaces. The ISS also features large solar arrays for power, docking ports for visiting spacecraft, and robotic arms for external repairs and assembly. The modular design allows for future expansion and upgrades, ensuring that the ISS remains adaptable to new scientific and exploratory objectives.
