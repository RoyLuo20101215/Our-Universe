Terrestrial Planets

Overview

Terrestrial planets are solid worlds with rocky surfaces and dense metal cores, sculpted by volcanic activity, tectonics, and erosion over billions of years. They are the closest planets to the Sun, each harboring unique landscapes shaped by their distinct atmospheres and histories

History

Observations of terrestrial planets go back thousands of years to early astronomers who studied Mars, Venus, and Mercury as wandering "stars." However, telescopic advancements in the 17th century allowed astronomers like Galileo to observe details on these planets’ surfaces. For example, Galileo's discovery of the phases of Venus supported the heliocentric model, transforming our understanding of the solar system. Detailed mapping and exploration of terrestrial planets gained momentum with space probes in the mid-20th century, including missions like Mariner to Mars and Venus.

Formation

Terrestrial planets formed from dense materials left near the Sun after the solar nebula began to coalesce. Over time, smaller rock and metal particles collided and merged, creating planetesimals. Through accretion, these planetesimals gradually grew larger, forming the rocky cores of what would become Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars. These planets' proximity to the Sun prevented lighter gases from accumulating, resulting in dense, rocky surfaces.

Current Investigations and Research

Studies on terrestrial planets focus heavily on Mars, with ongoing missions like NASA's Perseverance rover and the European Space Agency's ExoMars rover searching for signs of ancient life and analyzing soil composition to understand past habitability. Researchers are also investigating planetary atmospheres and surface conditions on Venus and Mercury, with a strong interest in exploring Venus’s extreme greenhouse effect to better understand atmospheric dynamics and climate change potential on Earth-like planets.
